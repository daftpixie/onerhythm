## Messaging foundations

### The ideology

> In a world often divided, OneRhythm brings people together to help fight the invisible bears of arrhythmia.

This line is the most important “re-alignment anchor.” Every major surface should reinforce it with some variation of:

- **visibility:** “What people carry becomes visible.”
- **community:** “No one fights alone.”
- **education:** “Truth you can act on—without diagnosis.”

The movement must feel **pro-human and pro-care**. The targeted “gap” is not “bad clinicians.” The gap is **system design**: the lived psychological load of arrhythmia is common, but it is inconsistently screened and supported in routine care—something that major professional consensus statements now explicitly advocate improving. citeturn0search0turn0search4

### The audience matrix

The site copy should recognize four primary identities (each should feel addressed without the experience fragmenting):

- **Patients:** “You are not overreacting. Your nervous system is doing what it was built to do.”
- **Caregivers / partners:** “You’re carrying it too. You deserve support and clarity.”
- **Supporters / advocates:** “You can help turn silence into visibility—without needing to be a patient.”
- **Clinicians / institutions:** “This is not an attack. It’s an invitation to complete arrhythmia care.” fileciteturn5file0

A key insight: the movement is broadest when participation isn’t limited to patient identity. That’s consistent with your platform’s synthetic-only approach: **anyone can join the mission** and still contribute meaningfully.

### The three pillars as public promises

Do not present these as “features.” Present them as “promises.”

- **We make it visible:** the shared rhythm distance turns private struggle into public proof.
- **We make it shared:** the mission is a collective artifact, owned by no one, grown by everyone.
- **We make it usable:** ResearchPulse translates evidence into human language so patients and clinicians have a common reference point. fileciteturn5file2

## Voice, tone, and language rules

### Tone constraints

The voice must feel:

- **trauma-informed:** validate fear without amplifying panic.
- **non-clinical:** hospitable, human, never hospital UI.
- **evidence-forward:** cite sources, name uncertainty where it exists, avoid overclaiming.
- **solution-forward:** explicitly honor EP/cardiology progress while calling for mental-health integration. fileciteturn5file0 citeturn0search0

Practically, this means avoiding “gotcha” rhetoric like:
- “The system is failing you.”
- “Doctors ignore mental health.”

Replace with language like:
- “Many clinics don’t yet have the tools or pathways.”
- “This is the next layer of complete arrhythmia care.”
- “We can make it easier to screen, refer, and support.” citeturn0search0

### Language swaps that should be applied broadly

From the Shared Rhythm Distance executive framing, the most strategic language shift is “shared” over “collected,” and “journey” over “dataset.” fileciteturn5file2

Recommended swaps (make these global website conventions):

- “We collected data” → “We shared rhythms”
- “Users submitted” → “People joined”
- “Contribution created” → “Your rhythm joined the mission”
- “Analytics” → “Visibility” or “What’s becoming visible”
- “Patients” (as default label) → “People living with arrhythmia” (person-first)
- “Not diagnostic” → “This does not diagnose or interpret ECGs. It’s solidarity + education.”

### Emotional cadence: avoid sadness-only

If you want shareability *and* dignity, don’t over-index on despair. Research on virality suggests high-arousal emotions (awe, anger, anxiety) can increase sharing, while low-arousal emotions like sadness are less likely to spread. The opportunity is to move people through a **three-beat arc**: recognition → urgency → hope. citeturn4search0

Recommended emotional arc for most pages:

1. **Recognition:** “Yes, this burden is real.”
2. **Urgency (without panic):** “The scale is larger than most people realize.”
3. **Hope + action:** “Here’s what we can do together—today.”

## Evidence and data strategy for mission-aligned visibility

Your site’s data points must feel **clinically credible** and **ethically careful**. The goal is not “shock value.” The goal is: “this is common enough that it deserves routine pathways.”

### The flagship mental-health statistics and how to use them safely

These are your “load-bearing” statistics. They should appear sparingly and consistently, with context:

- **Arrhythmia clinic distress prevalence (cross-sectional):** In a cohort of 222 arrhythmia clinic patients, participants reported moderate-to-extremely severe anxiety (88.3%), depression (71.1%), and stress (51%) on DASS-21 screening. Present this as “in one clinic cohort” and explicitly label it as screening-based and context-specific. citeturn2view0
- **ICD populations (meta-analysis):** A systematic review/meta-analysis of 39,954 ICD patients estimated clinically relevant anxiety at 22.58%, depression at 15.42%, and PTSD at 12.43%, with shocks associated with higher odds of anxiety and depression. Use this to justify trauma-informed post-shock support pathways. citeturn0search1turn0search3
- **Symptomatic AF in tertiary care:** In a tertiary AF cohort (n=78), severe psychological distress was present in 35% and suicidal ideation in 20%; effective AF ablation was associated with reductions in distress and suicidal ideation prevalence. Use this with respectful framing and crisis safety standards. citeturn5search1turn5search2
- **Social connection as cardiovascular-relevant:** A prospective UK Biobank multistate analysis found loneliness and social isolation associated with higher incidence and worse prognosis of AF, supporting the premise that enhancing connection is not just “engagement,” but potentially relevant to cardiovascular trajectories. citeturn3search0turn3search1turn3search3
- **Consensus direction for integration:** The 2025 ESC clinical consensus statement explicitly advocates integrating mental health into person-centered cardiovascular care through multidisciplinary “Psycho-Cardio” collaboration and practical implementation principles. Use this as the “permission structure” whenever the site speaks to institutions. citeturn0search0turn0search4

### Rules for using mental-health numbers in UI

Use these as “design constraints” Codex must follow when rewriting copy:

- **One page, one “hero stat”**: do not stack multiple distress/suicide/PTSD numbers on a single screen. The cumulative effect becomes emotionally coercive and can feel extractive.
- **Always pair a stat with an action:** “Here’s what helps” (screening pathways, referral, community support, evidence translation).
- **Always contextualize suicidality:** If a page mentions suicidal ideation or self-harm risk, it must also contain crisis support language and a clear “this isn’t therapy” boundary.
- **Never imply diagnosis:** Stats exist to motivate supportive care changes, not to suggest “your rhythm means you have X.”
- **Use citations in ResearchPulse, not heavy footnotes everywhere:** The main UI should feel humane. ResearchPulse pages can carry the full citations and methods.

### “Supportive to resolution” framing templates

Use these sentence patterns to keep the site from sounding accusatory:

- “Many arrhythmia clinics are not yet equipped with routine distress screening and referral pathways. We’re building visibility and practical tools to make that integration easier.” citeturn0search0

## Web IA messaging map

This section defines what each major surface should *mean*, not just what it should do. It’s intent-first so Codex can rewrite copy without sounding like PRD text.

### Homepage

**Primary intent:** A welcoming mission invitation that makes the movement legible in 10 seconds.

**Hero copy requirements:**
- Lead with unity: “In a world often divided…”
- Name the invisible bears without melodrama.
- Give one mission metric and one human promise:
  - “X km traveled”
  - “You are not alone”

**Recommended hero structure:**
- Headline: “Every rhythm shared. Every bear seen.”
- Subhead: “Join a global mission making the mental-health burden of arrhythmia visible—together.”
- Proof: live counters (distance traveled, countries represented)
- CTA: “Join the mission” / “See the mission”

**Stat placement (homepage):**
- Use a single rotating stat card maximum (weekly rotation).
- Default stat should be “high distress is common” (avoid suicidality on homepage by default).

### Join flow

**Primary intent:** Make joining feel like solidarity, not registration.

**Copy requirements:**
- Do not say “create contribution,” “submit,” “data.”
- Say “Join the mission,” “Add your rhythm,” “Light the path.”
- Explain (briefly) why email verification exists: “One rhythm per person, so the mission stays honest.”

**Microcopy pattern:**
- “Create your rhythm in seconds.”
- “Verify once to officially add your 0.75 m to the mission.”
- “No diagnosis. No interpretation. Just solidarity + truth.”

### Result page

**Primary intent:** Immediate reward + share amplification without guilt.

**What the user must feel:**
- “I did something meaningful.”
- “This is beautiful.”
- “Sharing is inviting, not pressuring.”

**Copy requirements:**
- Show the 0.75 m delta as a ceremonial moment.
- Tie to shared progress: “Together we’ve traveled…”
- Provide “share captions” that are short, dignified, and avoid clinical claims.

**Stat placement (result):**
- If showing mental-health stats, use one and tie it to “why this matters,” then offer “learn more in ResearchPulse.”

### Mission page

**Primary intent:** Mission-control awe + clarity + credibility.

**Country analytics framing:**

### ResearchPulse

## Content systems and cadence

### The core weekly content loop

This is the web’s “breathing pattern,” not social automation.

- **One rotating “Visibility Card” per week:** one stat, one sentence of meaning, one link to a ResearchPulse brief.
- **One “Milestone Story” per milestone:** short geographic metaphor + gratitude + invitation.
- **One ResearchPulse publication cadence:** consistent, not overwhelming; quality and clarity over volume.
- **One community encouragement post:** coping, questions to ask, how to talk to family, what post-shock fear can feel like.

### Guardrails against “data extraction” perception

Even though the platform is synthetic-only, the movement can still accidentally feel extractive if the user feels used for virality.

To prevent that, preserve these principles:

- **Immediate value returned:** created art, mission delta, and a clear next helpful action.
- **Agency:** optional name/country/note; transparent visibility settings.
- **Dignity:** no forced trauma storytelling.
- **Transparency:** clear statement: “We don’t diagnose. We don’t interpret ECGs. This is symbolic participation + education.”

## Governance and safety for mental-health messaging

Because the campaign intentionally highlights psychological burden, the site must treat mental-health messaging as a safety-sensitive domain.

Minimum standards to preserve everywhere:

- **Crisis resources are easy to find** and appear automatically on pages that mention suicidal ideation, self-harm, or “ending it.”
- **Avoid coercive framing** (“share to save lives”) on the result page; use invitational framing (“If this helps you feel less alone, share it.”).
- **Avoid lived-experience triggers**: do not use graphic language or imagery that evokes medical emergencies.

This approach keeps the movement bold and credible: it makes the invisible visible, without making pain into propaganda.

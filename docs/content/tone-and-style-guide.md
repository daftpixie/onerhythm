# Tone And Style Guide

This document defines the public writing rules for OneRhythm content.

## Core principle

Lead with data, land with humanity.

That means:

- name the reality clearly
- support it with reviewed sources when appropriate
- end in language a real person can carry with them

## Voice

OneRhythm should sound like:

- warm
- precise
- emotionally literate
- steady
- evidence-aware

It should not sound:

- clinical
- alarmist
- sales-driven
- self-congratulatory
- vague in order to sound “safe”

## Required writing posture

- Be direct about the problem.
- Be careful about certainty.
- Keep the educational boundary visible.
- Speak to people with lived experience, not abstract “patients.”
- Preserve dignity and privacy.

## Writing rules for educational content

Educational pages must:

- stay non-diagnostic
- keep the persistent disclaimer
- avoid treatment recommendations
- use plain language
- explain uncertainty when it matters

Prefer:

- “what this page explains”
- “what the research shows”
- “what it may mean in plain language”
- “questions to bring to your doctor”

Avoid:

- “we detected”
- “your ECG shows”
- “this proves”
- “you should start”
- “you likely have”

## Writing rules for campaign and advocacy content

Campaign pages may be more emotionally direct, but they still must:

- avoid spectacle
- avoid unsupported claims
- keep the public mission clear
- distinguish narrative from evidence

The goal is not to perform pain. The goal is to make an under-acknowledged burden visible with dignity.

## Writing rules for community stories

Story editing should:

- preserve the author’s emotional truth
- reduce privacy risk
- remove medical-advice framing
- avoid flattening the voice into institutional language

## Style choices

- Prefer short, clear sentences over dense blocks.
- Use humane verbs and concrete nouns.
- Avoid euphemistic safety language that hides the real point.
- Avoid startup hype, miracle framing, or “revolutionary” claims.
- Avoid overexplaining obvious disclaimers in body copy; let the persistent disclaimer do its job.

## Review questions

Before publishing, ask:

- Would a reader feel respected by this page?
- Is the source of authority clear?
- Does the copy avoid sounding like diagnosis or treatment advice?
- Does the page feel like OneRhythm rather than a hospital brochure or startup landing page?

## When to escalate review

Request extra review when content touches:

- triage-like language
- prognosis or outcomes
- treatment comparisons
- clinical risk framing
- acute mental health or crisis language

AGENTS requires high review sensitivity for medical-adjacent text. Follow that rule here too.
